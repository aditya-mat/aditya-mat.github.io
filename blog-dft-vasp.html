Getting Started with DFT: A Beginner's Notes on VASP

11 Jul 2026 · 3 min read
DFT VASP Tutorial

When I first heard the phrase "density functional theory," it sounded like something you needed a decade of physics to touch. A year into using it almost daily, I've realized the core idea is surprisingly graspable — and the confusion mostly comes from how it's usually taught. So here are the notes I wish someone had handed me on day one.

The problem DFT actually solves

Everything about a material — why it's a metal or an insulator, what color it is, how it bends light or stores charge — comes down to how its electrons behave. In principle, quantum mechanics tells us exactly how: solve the Schrödinger equation for every electron and you have your answer.

The catch is that electrons interact with each other. Every electron feels every other electron, so you can't solve for one without already knowing all the rest. For a real material with billions of electrons, this is hopeless. The math simply doesn't scale.

The clever trick

Here's the insight that earned Walter Kohn a Nobel Prize. Instead of tracking every electron and its tangle of interactions, you can describe the system entirely through the electron density — a single function that tells you how much electron "cloud" sits at each point in space.

That's a massive simplification. A wavefunction for many electrons lives in an enormous abstract space; the density is just a quantity you could, in principle, plot in three dimensions. DFT proves that this density contains everything you need to know about the ground state. Nothing is lost.

Kohn–Sham: the honest workaround

Of course, there's a price. We don't actually know the exact recipe connecting the density to the energy — one piece, called the exchange-correlation energy, has to be approximated. This is where DFT stops being exact and starts being a craft.

The Kohn–Sham equations reframe the impossible interacting problem as a set of non-interacting electrons moving in a cleverly designed effective potential. Each fictitious electron is easy to solve for on its own, and together they reproduce the true density. It's a bit of a magic trick: pretend the electrons don't talk to each other, but bake their conversation into the background potential they all feel.

Why it has to loop: self-consistency

There's a chicken-and-egg problem hiding here. The effective potential depends on the electron density — but the density is what you're trying to find in the first place. You can't have one without the other.

The solution is to iterate. You guess a starting density, build the potential from it, solve the Kohn–Sham equations to get a new density, and compare. If they don't match, you update and go again. Repeat until the density stops changing — at which point it's self-consistent with the potential it generates. That loop is the beating heart of every DFT calculation, and watching it converge for the first time is oddly satisfying.

Setting up my first VASP run

VASP turns all this theory into four input files. Once they clicked, everything felt less mysterious:

  • POSCAR — the atoms and where they sit (your crystal structure).
  • POTCAR — how each atomic species behaves (the pseudopotentials).
  • KPOINTS — how finely you sample the crystal's repeating pattern.
  • INCAR — the control panel: accuracy, the loop settings, what to calculate.

Get those four right and hit run, and the self-consistency loop I described above springs to life in your terminal.

What's next

This barely scratches the surface — I haven't touched convergence testing, functionals, or the endless art of reading an OUTCAR. But the conceptual scaffold above is what finally made DFT feel like a tool rather than a black box. More notes to come as I keep learning.
